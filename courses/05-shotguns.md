Pump, semiauto, and break-action shotguns; shot vs slug; and an overview of other types (e.g., muzzleloaders, NFA items) where they fit in.

## What makes a shotgun different

Shotguns are typically **smoothbore** (no rifling), though rifled barrels exist for slugs. They fire either **shot**—many small pellets (shotgun shot) that spread in a pattern—or a **slug**, a single heavy projectile. That versatility makes them the standard for bird hunting, clay sports, and many home-defense and hunting roles. Bore size is described in **gauge** (e.g., 12, 20); smaller number = larger bore.

## Pump-action shotguns

The shooter manually slides the forend rearward to eject the fired shell and forward to chamber the next. Reliable, simple, and tolerant of different loads and conditions.

- **Strengths** — No gas system or recoil spring to tune; they cycle almost any 12- or 20-gauge load. Inexpensive, easy to maintain, and iconic (e.g., "racking" the pump as a deterrent). Good for mixed use: light target loads one day, heavy buckshot the next.
- **Limitations** — Slower than a semiauto; you must remember to pump. Short-stroking (not fully cycling) causes malfunctions until you train the motion.
- **Typical uses** — Home defense, hunting (birds, turkey, deer with slug), tactical and training. The pump shotgun is the classic American utility long gun.
- **Famous examples** — Remington 870, Mossberg 500/590, Winchester SXP, Benelli Nova.

## Semiautomatic shotguns

One trigger pull fires one shot; the gun uses gas or recoil to eject the shell and chamber the next. No manual pump.

- **Strengths** — Faster follow-up shots and less perceived recoil (the action absorbs some of it). Preferred for clay games and waterfowl where you're firing multiple rounds quickly.
- **Limitations** — More sensitive to ammunition. Light loads may not cycle; heavy loads may batter the gun. Gas systems need cleaning. Usually more expensive than pumps.
- **Typical uses** — Sporting clays, skeet, trap, waterfowl and upland hunting, and any role where speed and reduced recoil matter.
- **Famous examples** — Benelli M2/M4 (inertia-driven), Beretta A300/A400 (gas), Remington 1100, Browning Auto-5, Mossberg 930.

## Break-action (single- and double-barrel)

The barrel(s) hinge at the breech; you "break" the gun open to load and eject. **Single-shot** has one barrel; **double guns** have two (side-by-side or over/under), often with two triggers or one trigger that selects the barrel.

- **Strengths** — Simple, safe (open action is obviously unloaded), and often beautiful. Over/unders dominate high-end clay shooting. No feeding mechanism to malfunction.
- **Limitations** — One or two shots before reloading. Heavier and more expensive per shot than repeaters for many applications.
- **Typical uses** — Trap, skeet, sporting clays, upland hunting, and traditional or collectible use.
- **Famous examples** — Beretta over/unders, Browning Citori, various side-by-sides (e.g., Stoeger, CZ).

## Shot vs slug

- **Shot** — A shell full of small pellets (birdshot, buckshot). Spread increases with distance; pattern depends on choke (cylinder, improved cylinder, modified, full). Used for birds, small game, clays, and at close range for defense (buckshot).
- **Slug** — A single heavy projectile. Used for deer and large game, and for defense where over-penetration is a concern (slugs penetrate deeply). **Rifled slugs** are for smoothbore barrels; **sabot slugs** are for rifled shotgun barrels for better accuracy.

Choke (built-in or screw-in) constricts the muzzle to tighten or widen the shot pattern. Tighter choke = tighter pattern at longer range; looser choke = wider pattern for close or fast targets.

## Other types: muzzleloaders and NFA

- **Muzzleloaders** — Loaded from the muzzle (black powder or substitute, projectile, and often a cap or primer). Used for traditional hunting, reenactment, and in some states for special hunting seasons. Require their own safety and cleaning routines.
- **NFA items** — In the U.S., short-barreled shotguns (e.g., barrel under 18"), and other "NFA" firearms are heavily regulated. Know federal and state law before buying or building.

Shotguns are uniquely versatile: same gun can shoot clays, birds, deer (with slug), and defensive loads. Choosing pump, semiauto, or break-action is about budget, use, and preference—all three are here to stay.
