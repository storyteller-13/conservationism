A firearm is any weapon that uses an explosive (usually gunpowder) to propel a projectile through a barrel. Understanding how firearms work, where they came from, and how they’re classified is the foundation of both responsible ownership and informed discussion.

## What is a gun?

At its core, a gun has three jobs: **contain** an explosion, **direct** the resulting gas and projectile down a tube (the barrel), and **control** when that happens (the trigger and action). The bullet or shot is pushed out at high speed; the rest of the gun is built to handle the pressure and recoil safely and to put the next round in place if the gun can fire again.

## A short history of firearms

- **Early gunpowder weapons (13th–14th c.)** — "Hand cannons" and early artillery used in China and Europe. The user lit the powder by hand; they were slow, dangerous, and inaccurate.
- **Matchlock (15th c.)** — A burning "match" (cord) was brought to the touch hole by a lever. This freed one hand to steady the gun and made aimed fire more practical. Dominant for about 200 years.
- **Wheel lock & flintlock (16th–18th c.)** — Sparks from a spinning wheel or a flint striking steel ignited the powder. No constantly burning match, so soldiers could stay ready without giving away their position. Flintlocks powered armies and frontiers well into the 19th century.
- **Percussion (mid-19th c.)** — A cap containing a shock-sensitive compound was struck by the hammer. More reliable in wet weather and faster to load. The percussion era overlapped with the first self-contained metallic cartridges.
- **Metallic cartridges (mid-19th c. onward)** — Bullet, powder, and primer in one brass (or other metal) case. This made repeating firearms practical: breech-loading rifles and revolvers could be reloaded quickly and could fire multiple rounds without hand-loading each time.
- **Repeating and semiautomatic (late 19th–20th c.)** — Lever- and bolt-action rifles, then semiautomatic pistols and rifles that use recoil or gas to cycle the action. One trigger pull = one shot, with the next round ready. Full-automatic (machine guns) followed the same mechanical ideas but allowed continuous fire while the trigger was held.

That arc—from hand-lit powder to self-loading, cartridge-fed arms—is the history of making firearms more reliable, accurate, and capable of sustained fire.

## How guns are classified

Firearms are grouped in several overlapping ways. Knowing these categories helps when reading laws, talking to other shooters, or choosing a first gun.

**By platform (what you hold)**

- **Handgun** — Designed to be fired with one or two hands, no shoulder stock. Includes revolvers and semiautomatic pistols.
- **Rifle** — Long gun with a rifled barrel (spiral grooves that spin the bullet for accuracy), usually fired from the shoulder. Used for hunting, target shooting, and defensive use at longer range.
- **Shotgun** — Long gun with a smoothbore (or sometimes rifled) barrel, firing a cluster of pellets (shot) or a single slug. Common for hunting birds and for home defense.

**By action (how the next round is readied)**

- **Single-shot** — One round at a time; the action must be opened and a new round loaded by hand after each shot.
- **Revolver** — Cylinder holds several rounds; rotating the cylinder (usually when the trigger is pulled or the hammer is cocked) brings the next round in line with the barrel.
- **Semiautomatic** — One shot per trigger pull; the gun uses recoil or gas to eject the empty case and load the next round. No manual cycling between shots.
- **Manual repeaters** — Bolt, lever, or pump action: the shooter cycles the action to eject and chamber the next round. Common on rifles and shotguns.
- **Fully automatic** — Continues firing while the trigger is held. Heavily regulated in most countries, including the U.S.

**By caliber and gauge**

Caliber describes the approximate bore diameter — either in inches (.22, .45) or millimeters (9mm, 7.62mm). 

Gauge is the shotgun equivalent: the number of lead balls of that bore diameter that together weigh one pound. A 12-gauge bore is larger than a 20-gauge bore.

Caliber and gauge determine what ammunition the gun uses and, broadly, what it's suited for (plinking, carry, hunting, long-range, etc.).
