Reading a headstamp, understanding caliber names, and choosing the right ammo for your gun and purpose.

## The basics of a cartridge

A modern **cartridge** (round) has four parts:

1. **Case** — Usually brass (sometimes steel or aluminum). Holds everything together and seals the chamber when the gun fires. Reusable for handloaders in many calibers.
2. **Primer** — A small cap at the base (centerfire) or in the rim (rimfire) that ignites when struck. The primer sets off the powder.
3. **Powder** — Propellant that burns and produces gas. The amount and type affect velocity and pressure.
4. **Bullet** (or shot/slug in shotguns) — The projectile that leaves the barrel. In rifles and handguns it's a single bullet; in shotguns the "bullet" might be many pellets or a slug.

**Rimfire vs centerfire** — **Rimfire** (e.g., .22 LR): the primer is in the rim of the case; the firing pin crushes the rim to ignite it. Cases aren't reloadable; small and low pressure. **Centerfire**: the primer is a separate cap in the center of the case head. Reloadable and used for almost all higher-power ammunition.

## Reading the headstamp

The **headstamp** is the marking on the base of the case. It usually shows:

- **Manufacturer or factory** — e.g., FC (Federal), WIN (Winchester), PMC, GFL (Fiocchi), or a NATO cross.
- **Caliber** — e.g., 9 mm, .45 AUTO, 5.56 NATO. Sometimes the caliber is implied by the cartridge family.
- **Other** — Year codes, batch numbers, or "NT" (non-toxic), "+P" (higher pressure), etc.

Headstamps help you identify what you're shooting and avoid mixing calibers. **Never assume a round is a certain caliber by looks alone**—always check the headstamp and the marking on the box. Using the wrong caliber in a gun can cause catastrophic failure and injury.

## Caliber names: what they mean

Caliber names are inconsistent by history, not by logic. They usually refer to **approximate bore or bullet diameter**, but the rest is convention.

- **Inches** — .22, .38, .45, .30-30, .45-70. The number is often the bore or bullet diameter in inches (.45 ≈ 0.45"). The second number (e.g., .30-30, .45-70) sometimes referred to powder charge or bullet weight in the black-powder era; today it's just part of the name.
- **Millimeters** — 9mm, 5.56mm, 7.62mm. Common in European and military designation. 9mm and 9mm Luger are the same; 5.56 NATO and .223 Remington are very close but not always identical (pressure differences—read your manual).
- **Same number, different cartridges** — .38 Special and .357 Magnum use bullets of similar diameter but different case length and power. .223 Remington and 5.56 NATO are often interchangeable in modern rifles but check the barrel marking. Never substitute without verifying.

If in doubt, use only ammunition that is **explicitly listed** for your firearm in the owner's manual.

## Choosing the right ammo

- **For your gun** — The barrel or slide is usually marked with the caliber (e.g., "9 mm", ".40 S&W"). The manual will list acceptable cartridges. Use only what is specified. "Plus" or "+P" loads are higher pressure; use only if the gun is rated for them.
- **For the task** — **Target/plinking:** full metal jacket (FMJ) or similar cheap ammo. **Defensive:** jacketed hollow point (JHP) or other expanding design that penetrates adequately and transfers energy. **Hunting:** appropriate expanding or soft-point bullets for the game and range. **Reloads:** only from trusted sources; unknown reloads can be dangerous.
- **Quality and storage** — Stick to reputable manufacturers. Store ammo in a cool, dry place. Avoid corrosion (water, humidity) and long-term exposure to heat. Old or damaged ammo should be disposed of safely, not fired.

## Shotgun shells

Shotgun ammunition is described by **gauge**, **length** (e.g., 2¾", 3"), **shot size** (e.g., #7½ birdshot, 00 buck), and sometimes **payload** (e.g., 1 oz, 1⅛ oz). The headstamp will show gauge and length (e.g., "12 2¾"). Use only the length and gauge your gun is chambered for; firing a 3" shell in a 2¾"-only chamber is dangerous.

- **Birdshot** — Small pellets for birds and clays. Numbers like #7½, #8; smaller number = larger pellet.
- **Buckshot** — Larger pellets for defense or large game. 00 buck is common for defense.
- **Slugs** — Single projectile. Rifled slugs for smoothbore; sabot slugs for rifled shotgun barrels.

Ammunition is the link between your gun and the target. Match it to the firearm and the purpose, and you'll shoot safely and effectively.
